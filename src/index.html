Your guide to Sound Transit 3: What you'd pay, what you'd get

Here’s the rundown on 12 major projects promised in the Sound Transit 3 measure

By Mike Lindblom, Thomas Wilburn and Kelly Shea
Originally published October 21, 2016
Map of projects | Other features | Funding breakdown | Pros and Cons

The $54 billion ballot measure, in urban Snohomish, King and Pierce counties, calls for these increases (which do not include tax for other transit agencies):

  • Property tax, $25 a year per $100,000 of assessed value.
  • Sales tax, increase of 50 cents per $100 purchase.
  • Car-tab tax, a yearly increase of $80 per $10,000 vehicle value.

A median household would pay $326 next year per household in new tax (or $169 per adult), plus $303 in existing Sound Transit taxes that voters passed in 1996 and 2008. Actual costs vary by income and lifestyle, so try this ST3 tax calculator to find your own tax increase.

Besides rail and bus extensions, the plan proposes:
  • Housing A $20 million fund for low-interest revolving loans to be used by housing developers who set low rents. Four-fifths of surplus land must be used for affordable units after Sound Transit construction teams are finished using it. Another $30 million is earmarked to study or plan dense “transit-oriented development.”
  • Payments to the state A 2015 law requires $518 million from ST3 tax revenues be paid to the state general fund for education or social services.
  • Fleet replacement fund An estimated $781 million would be set aside through 2041 for to replace trains, tracks and station equipment — to prevent breakdowns like those in Washington, D.C.
  • Near-term bus projects To keep ST Express, Community Transit, and RapidRide moving, bus lanes would be added, including on highway shoulders. Also promised are more service hours, a Sammamish park-and-ride, and money for a Madison Street bus corridor in Seattle.
  • Planning for ST4 A total $187 million to plan a fourth round of projects and study future corridors, including White Center, Burien, Renton, north Everett, Orting, Tacoma Mall, a crosstown Ballard-UW line, and north Lake Washington.
Source: Sound Transit 3 guide, Appendix A

Sound Transit Proposition 1, pro and con

In favor

  • The region needs more transportation capacity, for today’s trips and 800,000 people expcted to move here by 2040.
  • Taxpayers in places such as Everett, Ballard, West Seattle, Federal Way, Kirkland and Issaquah have been paying for light-rail elsewhere for years. It’s their turn.
  • Frequent four-car trains can move 16,000 people an hour, while a general highway lane carries 2,000 vehicles — and there’s no room to expand freeways.
  • Light-rail “lets you escape congestion, reduces climate pollution, and improves access and affordability for all.”
  • Projects would create tens of thousands of skilled construction jobs, for a quarter-century.

Against

  • Measure would triple Sound Transit’s car-tab tax — bringing the total for ST3 plus earlier packages to $1,000 a year for “middle-class families.”
  • ST3 wouldn’t reduce congestion. Rail serves a fraction of regional trips, and “just moves people who already ride buses onto trains.”
  • Bus-rapid transit lanes could be established sooner at far less cost.
  • For the first time, Sound Transit seeks a property tax, which could make it tougher for the Legislature to increase education funding.
  • Autonomous cars, clean electric vehicles, and ride-service companies such as Uber and Lyft would make light rail obsolete, before the rail extensions are finished.
Sources: King County Voters Guide, Seattle Times archives
